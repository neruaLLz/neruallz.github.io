Technical Writing:

  • Definition: a form of written document providing technical information that helps readers to solve complex problems
  • Main features:
  1. Reader-centeredness
  2. Clear organization and page design
  3. Readable style and effective visuals
  • Purposes:
  1. to inform (provide information that answers questions)
  2. to instruct (help people do something)
  3. to persuade (encourage to take a desired action or make a decision)
  • Audiences: people who are reading the material in order to do something or learn something

Audiences:

  • Who:
  1. Primary audience: immediate readers - make decisions
  2. Secondary audience: those outside the immediate circles - advise the decision makers
  • Why:
  1. Primary purpose: to inform, instruct and persuade
  2. Secondary purpose: (when planning your document, start with a clear statement that identifies the target audience as well as the document’s primaru and secondary purposes.)
  • How: Adapt your writing to your audiences’ knowledge, interests and needs. Access your audiences’ technical background before deciding your document should be highly technical (for experts), semitechnical (for imformed persons) or nontechnical (for laypersons). ↓
Who will use the documents At what level of technicality How much do these readers need
Experts Highly technical Just the facts & figures
Informed persons Semitechnical Facts & figures explained
Laypersons Nontechnical Facts & figures explained in simplest terms

In general, a 2-10 point difference of type size between the headings and the body is acceptable.

Process for writing technical documents

  1. Planning
  2. Drafting
  3. Revising
  4. Editing
  5. Proofreading

Planning

  • Analyze your audience
    • Who are primary and secondary audiences?
    • What are their technical backgrounds and expectations?
  • Analyze your purpose
    • Why and how will your readers use the document?
    • After they have read the document, what do you want them to know or to do?
  • Generate ideas about your subject
    • Ask questions about who, what, when, where, why and how.
    • brainstorm to produce as many ideas as possible
  • Research additional information
    • Know how to collect, evaluate and document the right materials from primary and secondary sources
  • Organize and outline your document
  • Select a design or a delivery method
    • Design refers to the physical appearance of documents and websites.
  • Devise a schedule and a budget

Drafting

× The order in which you write does not matter. ✔ What does matter is the order in which the reader reads.

Revising

  • When revising, you read your documents and make changes to word, organization and word choice.
  • Start with thr general content, and then move to the details.

Editing

  • Improve grammar, punctuation, style, usage, diction and mechanics.
    • Diction: word choice
    • Mechanics: matters such as the use of numbers and abbreviations

Proofreading

  • Check to make sure you have typed what you meant to type.
  • Don’t rely on the spell-checker or grammar-checker.

Readable writing style for technical documents

  1. Clarity
    • Use active voice whenever possible
    • Avoid overstuffed sentences
  2. Conciseness
    • Use fewer words whenever fewer will do.
  3. Fluency
    • Provide clear connections, variety and emphasis.
  4. Appropriate tone

Five major goals of designing technical documents:

  • To make a good impression on readers
  • To help readers understand the structure and hierarchy of the information
  • To help readers find the information they need
  • To help readers understand the information
  • To help readers remember the informations

Four major principles of design (interconnected):

  • Proximity (related items should be grouped together)
  • Alignment (consciously place texts and graphics on the page - center/left/right-aligned)
  • Repetition (treat the same kind of information in the same way to create consistent patterns)
  • Contrast (real differences in color, font type, font size, spacing, graphic size)

Types of visuals

  • Table
    • display organized data across columns and easy for easy comparison
    • numeric tables (compare exact values) vs prose tables (organize verbal information)
  • Graph
    • translate numbers into shapes, shades, and patterns
    • bar graphs (show comparison) vs line graphs (show a trend over time)
  • Chart
    • depict relationships via geometric arrows, lines, and other design elements
    • flowcharts (trace the steps or decisions in a procedure or process), gantt charts (depict how the phases of a project relate), tree charts (show how the parts of an idea or concept relate), pictograms (use icons or other graphic devices that represent the displayed items)
  • Graphic illustration
    • rely on pictures rather than on data or words

Suggestions for designing documents:

  • Shaping the page
  • Styling the words and letters
  • Adding emphasis
  • Using headings for access and orientation

Shaping the page

  • provide page numbers, headers and footers
  • use a grid
  • use white space to create areas of emphasis
  • make lists for easy reading

Styling the words and letters

  • select an appropriate typeface (serif or sans serif)
    • serif: for newspapers or formal reports
    • sans serif: for technical materials (like numebers, equations, for marginal comments, headings, examples, tables, and captions) and projected environments (like overhead transparencies and PowerPoint slides)
  • use full caps sparingly

Adding emphasis (be consistent)

  • indention
  • lines
  • boldface
  • type sizes
  • color

Using heading for access and orientation

  • keep headings visually consistent and grammatically parallel

Outlining instructions

  • Preface your instructions with a title page
    • topic (iPod -> Operating Instructions for the iPod)
    • purpose
    • graphic
  • Safety requirements (anywhere throughout the text, but before a step)
  • Table of contents
  • Introduction
  • Required tools or equipment
  • Glossary
    • define abbreviations, acronyms, technical terms
  • Instructional steps
    • the most important part of your manual
  • Additional components
    • technical specifications
    • warranties
    • disclaimers
    • frequently asked questions
    • corporate contact information
      • company’s address, telephone number, fax number, email address, social media links
  • Graphics

Guidelines for drafting steps/writing insturctions

  1. Organize the steps chronologically.
  2. Number the instructions.
  3. Present the right amount of information in each step.
    • Each step should define a single task that readers can carry out easily.
  4. Begin your steps with an active-voice in the imperative mood.
  5. Do not omit articles.
  6. Develop your points thoroughly.
  7. Use short words, short sentences, and short paragraphs.
  8. Make sure each step is truly a step.
    • The emphasis is on the action, what readers need to do, instead of a description.
  9. Include graphics.

After you finish drafting the step-by-step instructions, you must test the accuracy and clarity of the steps before you submit your final copy.

Planning for safety

If readers will encounter safety risks, explain:

  • what those risks are
  • how to minimize them
    • be conservative – put safety information wherever you think readers are likely to see it
    • don’t be afraid to repeat yourself – a reasonable amount of repetition is effective
    • alert your readers to any potentially hazardous materials or acations before they reach certain steps
    • caution readers about any requirements for special clothing, tools, equipment. and other safety measure
    • highlight warnings, cautions and precautions to make them stand out visually from the surrounding text

Tips for providing effective safety information:

  • Access - making the hazard notations obvious
    • vary your typeface and type size
    • use white space to separate the warning / caution from surrounding text
    • box the warning or caution
    • call attention to the hazards through graphics
  • Colors - emphasizing hazard messages
    • usually, Note is printed in blue or black, Caution in yellow, Warning in orange, and Danger in red.
  • Text - clarifying the terminology, containing:
    • an identification (1-2 words): “High voltage” or “High equipment”, “Sharp objects”
    • the consequences of the hazards (3-5 words): “Can cause burns”, “Cuts can occur”
    • avoidance steps (3-5 words): “Wear rubber shoes”, “Don’t touch until cool”, “Wear protective gloves”
  • Icon - helping everyone understand the caution, warning or danger
